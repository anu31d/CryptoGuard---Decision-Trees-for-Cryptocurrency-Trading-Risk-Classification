🪙 Cryptocurrency Basics

Cryptocurrency

A digital or virtual currency that uses cryptography for security. Unlike traditional currencies, cryptocurrencies operate on decentralized networks based on blockchain technology, making them resistant to government interference or manipulation.

Bitcoin (BTC)

The first and most well-known cryptocurrency, created in 2009 by an anonymous person or group using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto. It serves as digital gold and a store of value.

Altcoin

Any cryptocurrency other than Bitcoin. Examples include Ethereum, Cardano, Solana, and thousands of others. Each altcoin may have different features, use cases, and technologies.

Blockchain

A distributed digital ledger that records all transactions across a network of computers. Each block contains transaction data and is linked to previous blocks, creating an immutable chain of records.

Wallet

A digital tool that allows you to store, send, and receive cryptocurrencies. Wallets can be hardware devices (cold wallets) or software applications (hot wallets).

Token

A digital asset created on an existing blockchain (like Ethereum). Unlike coins which have their own blockchain, tokens are built using smart contracts on other platforms.

📈 Trading Terms

Market Price

The current price at which a cryptocurrency can be bought or sold. Market prices are determined by supply and demand dynamics across exchanges.

Open Price

The price of a cryptocurrency at the beginning of a trading period (usually a day). It represents the first trade executed when the market opens.

Close Price

The final price of a cryptocurrency at the end of a trading period. This is often used as the reference price for daily analysis and is shown in our analytics.

High Price

The highest price reached by a cryptocurrency during a specific time period. This indicates the peak buying pressure during that period.

Low Price

The lowest price reached by a cryptocurrency during a specific time period. This indicates the maximum selling pressure during that period.

Volume

The total amount of a cryptocurrency traded during a specific period. High volume indicates strong market interest, while low volume may suggest weak momentum.

Market Cap (Market Capitalization)

The total value of all coins in circulation, calculated by multiplying the current price by the total supply. It's used to rank cryptocurrencies by size.

Market Cap = Current Price × Circulating Supply

All-Time High (ATH)

The highest price a cryptocurrency has ever reached in its trading history. Investors often use ATH as a reference point for potential future growth.

All-Time Low (ATL)

The lowest price a cryptocurrency has ever reached. This can indicate strong support levels or historical market bottoms.

📊 Analysis Terms

Return

The percentage change in price over a period. Positive returns indicate profit, while negative returns indicate loss. Daily returns are used in our risk model.

Return = (Current Price - Previous Price) / Previous Price × 100%

Volatility

A measure of how much and how quickly a cryptocurrency's price changes. High volatility means prices can change dramatically in a short time, indicating higher risk but also higher potential reward.

Volatility = Standard Deviation of Returns

Standard Deviation

A statistical measure that shows how spread out the data is from the average. In crypto, it measures how much returns deviate from the mean return.

Rolling Volatility

Volatility calculated over a moving window (e.g., 7 days). This shows how volatility changes over time and is used in our analytics charts.

Mean (Average)

The sum of all values divided by the number of values. In our statistics table, we show the mean price and volume to understand typical market behavior.

Trend

The general direction in which a cryptocurrency's price is moving. Trends can be upward (bullish), downward (bearish), or sideways (consolidation).

⚠️ Risk Classification

Low Risk (Volatility < 2%)

Indicates stable market conditions with minimal price fluctuations. Suitable for conservative investors or when holding positions. The price is relatively predictable.

  • Price changes are small and gradual
  • Good for long-term holding
  • Lower potential for quick profits
  • Lower potential for sudden losses

Medium Risk (Volatility 2% - 5%)

Moderate market movement with manageable risk exposure. This is typical for most trading days in established cryptocurrencies.

  • Normal market conditions
  • Standard risk management applies
  • Balanced risk-reward ratio
  • Suitable for active traders

High Risk (Volatility > 5%)

Highly volatile conditions requiring caution. Prices can swing dramatically, leading to significant gains or losses in short periods.

  • Large price swings expected
  • Use stop-loss orders
  • Consider reducing position size
  • Often occurs during major news events

Decision Tree

A machine learning algorithm that makes decisions by splitting data based on feature values. Our model uses a decision tree to classify trading risk based on return, volatility, and volume change.

Classification

The process of categorizing data into predefined classes. Our model classifies trading conditions into three risk categories: Low, Medium, and High.

Feature Engineering

The process of creating meaningful input variables for a machine learning model. We engineer features like returns, volatility, and volume changes from raw price data.

🔧 Technology Terms

Decentralization

The distribution of power away from a central authority. In crypto, no single entity controls the network; instead, thousands of nodes maintain and verify the blockchain.

Mining

The process of using computer power to validate transactions and add new blocks to the blockchain. Miners are rewarded with cryptocurrency for their efforts.

Smart Contract

Self-executing code stored on a blockchain that automatically enforces the terms of an agreement. Ethereum popularized smart contracts, enabling DeFi and NFTs.

DeFi (Decentralized Finance)

Financial services built on blockchain technology without traditional intermediaries like banks. Includes lending, borrowing, trading, and earning interest on crypto.

Staking

Locking up cryptocurrency to support network operations (like validating transactions) in exchange for rewards. It's an alternative to mining in Proof-of-Stake blockchains.

Gas Fees

Transaction fees paid to process operations on a blockchain. On Ethereum, gas fees vary based on network congestion and transaction complexity.
